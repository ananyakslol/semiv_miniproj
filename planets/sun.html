SUN

The Star of the Solar System

Overview

The Sun is a massive ball of hot, glowing gas at the center of our solar system. It is the primary source of heat and light for all the planets. Without the Sun, life on Earth couldn’t exist. Its gravity is what holds the solar system together, keeping the planets, moons, asteroids, and comets in their orbits. Even though it looks small from Earth, the Sun is enormous — about 1.39 million kilometers wide. You could fit over 100 Earths across its diameter and over a million Earths inside its volume. The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (or "yellow dwarf") and is powered by nuclear fusion in its core. This process turns hydrogen into helium, releasing an enormous amount of energy in the form of light and heat. The Sun has several layers, each with different properties. The innermost core is where fusion happens. Surrounding it are the radiative and convective zones, where energy moves outward. The surface, or photosphere, is what we see from Earth. Above that are the chromosphere and corona, the Sun’s outer atmosphere, which can be seen during a solar eclipse as a glowing halo. The Sun goes through cycles of activity every 11 years, including increases and decreases in sunspots, solar flares, and coronal mass ejections. These events can affect space weather and sometimes interfere with satellite communications or power grids on Earth. Despite being so familiar, the Sun remains a focus of scientific study, especially regarding its influence on Earth and the possibility of solar storms.

My Name?

Earth is the only planet not named after a Greek or Roman deity. Its name comes from Old English and Germanic words meaning “ground” or “soil.”

Composition

Earth has a balanced structure that supports life: a breathable atmosphere, a solid surface with water and land, and a dense core that generates a magnetic field.

Atmosphere

78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 1% other gases (argon, CO₂, etc.)

Surface

Continents, oceans, forests, mountains, deserts, and polar ice caps

Core

Solid inner core and molten outer core of iron and nickel

Fun Facts

Magnetic Shield

Earth’s magnetic field, created by its spinning iron core, helps protect us from solar wind and cosmic radiation

Synchronised Spin

The Moon always shows the same face to Earth — it’s tidally locked, meaning its rotation period matches its orbit.

One Moon

Earth has one natural satellite — the Moon, which affects tides and stabilizes our tilt.
